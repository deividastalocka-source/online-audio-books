Summary

F. Scott Fitzgerald’s classic American novel of the Roaring Twenties is beloved by generations of readers and stands as his crowning work. This new audio edition, authorized by the Fitzgerald estate, is narrated by Oscar-nominated actor Jake Gyllenhaal (Brokeback Mountain). Gyllenhaal's performance is a faithful delivery in the voice of Nick Carraway, the Midwesterner turned New York bond salesman, who rents a small house next door to the mysterious millionaire Jay Gatsby.

There, he has a firsthand view of Gatsby’s lavish West Egg parties - and of his undying love for the beautiful Daisy Buchanan.After meeting and losing Daisy during the war, Gatsby has made himself fabulously wealthy. Now, he believes that his only way to true happiness is to find his way back into Daisy’s life, and he uses Nick to try to reach her. What happens when the characters’ fantasies are confronted with reality makes for a startling conclusion to this iconic masterpiece.This special audio edition joins the upcoming film - as well as many other movie, radio, theater, and even video-game adaptations - as a fitting tribute to the cultural significance of Fitzgerald’s Jazz Age classic, widely regarded as one of the greatest stories ever told.

Autobiography

F. Scott Fitzgerald (1896–1940) was an American novelist best known for The Great Gatsby (1925), a masterpiece that captured the extravagance and moral decline of the Jazz Age. Born in St. Paul, Minnesota, he briefly attended Princeton University before leaving to join the army during World War I. His debut novel, This Side of Paradise (1920), brought him instant fame and financial success, allowing him to marry Zelda Sayre. The couple became symbols of the Roaring Twenties, living extravagantly in New York and Paris while Fitzgerald continued to write, producing works like The Beautiful and Damned (1922) and Tender Is the Night (1934).

Despite his early success, Fitzgerald struggled with alcoholism and financial instability, while Zelda suffered from worsening mental illness. As his literary reputation declined, he moved to Hollywood in the late 1930s to work as a screenwriter but found little success. He died of a heart attack in 1940 at the age of 44, believing himself a failure. However, his work was rediscovered in later decades, and today he is regarded as one of the greatest American writers, with The Great Gatsby considered a defining novel of the 20th century.
